# ECRO-Forecasting

# Private Power-System Dataset Description

The private power-system datasets in this repository are used to support the event-conditioned residual operator learning experiments in the paper. The datasets are built around two typical forecasting tasks in power systems: user-side electricity consumption forecasting, including the Residential Electricity Consumption dataset (REC) and the Agricultural Electricity Consumption dataset (AEC); and electricity market price forecasting, including the Day-ahead Electricity Price dataset (DEP) and the Real-time Electricity Price dataset (REP).

These datasets are derived from real business records of a provincial power system and can reflect nonstationary variations in user-side electricity demand, electricity market prices, and external event-driven disturbances. In addition, we construct corresponding event text datasets for the above time series to characterize the impacts of external factors such as policy regulation, economic activities, extreme weather, holidays, and market operating conditions on future power-system dynamics.

This repository mainly provides dataset statistics, field definitions, preprocessing procedures, event text construction rules, and anonymized examples to help readers understand the composition, processing logic, and event semantic structuring process of the experimental data.

## 1. Dataset Overview

The private power-system datasets contain four subsets. The detailed statistics are as follows.

| Dataset | Full Name | Frequency | Target Variable | Timespan | Number of Samples | Data Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|---:|---|
| REC | Residential Electricity Consumption | Daily | Residential electricity consumption | 2023-01-01 to 2025-12-31 | 1096 | Private |
| AEC | Agricultural Electricity Consumption | Daily | Agricultural electricity consumption | 2023-01-01 to 2025-12-31 | 1096 | Private |
| DEP | Day-ahead Electricity Price | Hourly | Day-ahead electricity price | 2024-01-01 to 2025-06-15 | 12624 | Private |

REC and AEC are used to characterize daily electricity demand variations for different user types, while DEP and REP are used to characterize hourly price fluctuations in the electricity market. All four datasets exhibit clear temporal dependence, periodicity, and nonstationarity, and are affected by external events such as policies, weather, holidays, economic activities, and market supply-demand changes.

## 2. Numerical Time-Series Data

This study uses four private power-system time-series datasets, including two electricity price forecasting datasets and two electricity consumption forecasting datasets. DEP and REP correspond to day-ahead electricity price and real-time electricity price, respectively, while REC and AEC correspond to residential electricity consumption and agricultural electricity consumption, respectively. All numerical data are organized in chronological order, and the training, validation, and test sets are split according to time order to avoid future information leakage.

**DEP and REP: Electricity Price Datasets**

- **Data source and collection:** DEP and REP are derived from provincial day-ahead electricity price records and real-time electricity price records, respectively, both generated by electricity trading-related business systems. The two datasets cover the period from January 1, 2024 to June 15, 2025, with an hourly sampling frequency and a total of 12624 hourly samples. DEP reflects price changes formed in the day-ahead trading process, while REP reflects real-time price changes influenced by supply-demand conditions, market transactions, load fluctuations, and operational constraints during actual system operation.

- **Data characteristics:** DEP and REP are both hourly univariate time series. Each record corresponds to an hourly timestamp and its observed electricity price. The core fields include `timestamp` and `price`, where `timestamp` denotes the hourly timestamp and `price` denotes the electricity price at the corresponding time. The two datasets cover weekdays, weekends, holidays, seasonal transition periods, and peak electricity demand periods, reflecting intraday fluctuations, weekly periodicity, seasonal variations, and abnormal market fluctuations in electricity prices.

- **Data processing and quality control:** DEP and REP follow a unified preprocessing procedure. First, the original time fields are standardized, and a continuous hourly time index is constructed. Second, duplicate timestamps, missing hours, and abnormal records are checked to ensure that each hour corresponds to only one valid electricity price observation. Extreme high or low price points are not simply removed; instead, they are verified together with electricity market operating characteristics. If an abnormal fluctuation has a reasonable market explanation, such as supply-demand tension, trading volatility, holiday-related load changes, or extreme weather impacts, it is retained to reflect nonstationary changes in the electricity market.

**REC and AEC: Electricity Consumption Datasets**

- **Data source and collection:** REC and AEC are derived from residential-side and agricultural-side electricity consumption statistics of a provincial power company, respectively. The two datasets cover the period from January 1, 2023 to December 31, 2025, with a daily sampling frequency and a total of 1096 daily samples. REC is used to characterize daily electricity demand variations of residential users, while AEC is used to characterize electricity demand variations related to agricultural production.

- **Data characteristics:** REC and AEC are both daily univariate time series. Each record corresponds to one calendar day and its observed electricity consumption. Both datasets contain only one core target variable, namely the electricity consumption on the corresponding date. The core fields include `date` and `electricity_consumption`, where `date` denotes the calendar date and `electricity_consumption` denotes the electricity consumption on that date. REC reflects residential electricity consumption fluctuations under seasonal changes, holidays, and changes in daily living behavior. AEC reflects agricultural electricity consumption variations under seasonal production cycles, irrigation demand, farming activities, and related policy influences.

- **Data processing and quality control:** REC and AEC follow a unified preprocessing procedure. First, the original date fields are standardized, and a continuous daily time index is constructed. Second, time-series completeness, duplicate dates, and obvious abnormal records are checked to ensure that each calendar day retains only one valid electricity consumption observation. Individual abnormal consumption fluctuations are not mechanically removed; instead, they are judged together with holidays, agricultural production cycles, electricity usage behavior changes, and business context. If an abnormal fluctuation has a reasonable real-world explanation, it is retained to reflect nonstationary changes in real electricity demand.

## 3. Event Text Data

To support text-event-driven nonstationary time series forecasting, we construct corresponding event text datasets for REC, AEC, DEP, and REP. The relevant news, announcements, and public information are mainly collected from public sources such as People's Daily Online, the Shandong Provincial People's Government, and China Shandong Net. The collected texts cover external events that may affect power-system operation and electricity demand changes.

The event texts mainly include the following types:

- Policy regulation events, such as energy policies, electricity market rule adjustments, and energy-saving policies;
- Economic activity events, such as industrial production, consumption activities, and regional economic changes;
- Extreme weather events, such as high temperatures, cold waves, heavy rainfall, typhoons, and weather warnings;
- Holiday events, such as the Spring Festival, Labor Day, National Day, and Dragon Boat Festival;
- Social activity events, such as large-scale events, travel peaks, and public events;
- Energy supply-demand events, such as electricity supply-demand changes, energy structure adjustments, and market transaction fluctuations.

The raw texts are not directly fed into the forecasting model. Instead, they are converted into structured event variables through the text-to-event mapping process. This process reduces noise and redundant information in open texts and enables external events to establish clearer semantic correspondence with time-series forecasting windows.

---

**Text-to-Event Mapping Process**

The text-to-event mapping process includes the following steps.

- **Event collection:** Event texts that may affect electricity demand or electricity prices are collected from public news, government announcements, and authoritative media reports. The text sources include People's Daily Online, the Shandong Provincial People's Government, China Shandong Net, and other public channels.

- **Task relevance filtering:** Clearly irrelevant texts are filtered out according to the forecasting target, publication time, geographical scope, and event content. Only texts that may affect the target series through user behavior, market conditions, meteorological factors, policy adjustments, or economic activities are retained.

- **Event clustering and deduplication:** Open texts often contain repeated reports, reprinted news, and similar announcements. To avoid repeatedly feeding the same event into the model, candidate texts are clustered and deduplicated. Multiple similar texts are merged into one candidate event, and the text with more complete information and clearer expression is selected as the representative text.

- **Semantic structuring:** Candidate events are semantically structured by extracting fields such as impact direction, impact intensity, impact duration, impact factor, and causal explanation. The goal of this step is not to extract isolated keywords, but to convert open texts into structured semantic conditions that can be used for event response modeling.

- **Temporal alignment:** Structured events are aligned to the corresponding forecasting start time according to their publication time. The model is only allowed to use event information that has already been released before the forecasting time, thereby avoiding future information leakage.

---

**Examples of Event Structuring**

The following examples illustrate how raw event texts are converted into structured event variables. The examples are simplified based on public news or announcements and are used only to demonstrate the text-to-event mapping logic.

**Example 1: Extreme Weather Event for Residential Electricity Consumption**

Raw text:

> At 11:20 on June 7, 2024, the Shandong Meteorological Observatory issued an orange alert for high temperatures. Affected by the continental warm high-pressure ridge, inland areas of Shandong Province were expected to experience persistent temperatures above 37°C from June 9 to June 14, with maximum temperatures in some areas exceeding 40°C. This high-temperature weather event was characterized by long duration, wide coverage, and high intensity, and could have adverse impacts on urban and rural electricity use. Relevant precautions were recommended.

Structured event:

```json
{
  "title": "Shandong Province issued an orange alert for high temperatures",
  "publish_time": "2024-06-07 11:20:00",
  "direction": "positive",
  "intensity": "high",
  "duration": "short-term",
  "factor": "weather",
  "rationale": "Persistent high temperatures may increase residential cooling demand, raise short-term residential electricity consumption, and create temporary pressure on urban and rural electricity use."
}
```

**Example 2: Electricity Spot Market Event for Real-time Electricity Price**

Raw text:

> On June 17, 2024, the Shandong Regulatory Office of the National Energy Administration, together with the Shandong Provincial Development and Reform Commission and the Shandong Provincial Energy Administration, issued a notice stating that the Shandong electricity spot market would officially enter formal operation from that day. The electricity spot market can reflect power-grid supply-demand changes through price signals and guide market participants to take part in grid regulation.

Structured event:

```json
{
  "title": "Shandong electricity spot market entered formal operation",
  "publish_time": "2024-06-17 00:00:00",
  "direction": "positive",
  "intensity": "medium",
  "duration": "long-term",
  "factor": "market",
  "rationale": "The formal operation of the electricity spot market strengthens the reflection of supply-demand conditions, load fluctuations, and market transaction changes in real-time prices. During periods of tight supply-demand balance or high load, it may increase real-time electricity prices and enhance price volatility."
}
```

Due to the involvement of real power-system operation information, electricity market transaction records, and user-side electricity consumption statistics, the complete dataset is not publicly available for privacy protection and data security reasons. De-identified samples may be obtained by contacting the corresponding author and are limited to academic research and reproducibility studies.
